Understanding Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety disorders are a group of conditions characterized by excessive fear and anxiety, along with related behavioral disturbances. These disorders differ based on the specific objects or situations that trigger fear, anxiety, or avoidance behaviors, and the associated thoughts and feelings.

  • Fear: An emotional response to a real or perceived imminent threat, often associated with fight-or-flight responses.
  • Anxiety: Anticipation of a future threat, often linked to muscle tension, vigilance, and cautious or avoidant behaviors.

The developmental sequencing of anxiety disorders often begins with Separation Anxiety Disorder, followed by Specific Phobia, Social Anxiety Disorder, Panic Disorder, Agoraphobia, and Generalized Anxiety Disorder.

Specific Phobia

Specific Phobia involves a marked fear or anxiety about a specific object or situation (e.g., flying, heights, animals, injections, blood). This fear is often disproportionate to the actual danger and significantly interferes with daily life.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Marked fear/anxiety about a specific object or situation.
  • The phobic stimulus almost always provokes immediate fear/anxiety.
  • Active avoidance or endurance with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual danger.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Increased physiological arousal (except for blood-injection-injury type, which may cause vasovagal fainting).
  • Associated with abnormal activity in the amygdala, anterior cingulate cortex, thalamus, and insula.
  • Comorbidity with other anxiety, depressive, bipolar, substance-related, somatic symptom, and personality disorders.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is 8%-12%; generally lower in Asian, African, Latin American countries.
  • Women are more frequently affected (approx. 2:1 ratio).
  • Onset usually in early childhood (median 7-11 years).
  • Risk factors include negative affectivity, behavioral inhibition, parental overprotectiveness, and genetic susceptibility (e.g., specific phobia of animals).
  • Associated with suicidal thoughts and attempts.

Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)

Social Anxiety Disorder is characterized by marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations where the individual is exposed to possible scrutiny by others. The fear stems from concern about negative evaluation (e.g., humiliating or embarrassing oneself).

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Fear/anxiety in social situations involving scrutiny (e.g., conversations, eating, performing).
  • Fear of acting in a way or showing anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated.
  • Social situations almost always provoke fear/anxiety.
  • Avoided or endured with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual threat.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Individuals may be inadequately assertive, overly submissive, or overly controlling of conversation.
  • May show rigid body posture, inadequate eye contact, or soft voice.
  • Self-medication with substances is common. Blushing is a hallmark physical response.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is approximately 7%. Higher rates in women than men in the general population.
  • Onset does not differ by gender, but women report more fears and comorbidity.
  • Risk factors include behavioral inhibition, fear of negative evaluation, harm avoidance, negative social experiences (e.g., peer victimization, childhood maltreatment).
  • Modest genetic contribution (approx. 30% heritability).
  • Associated with elevated rates of school dropout, decreased well-being, employment, and quality of life.

Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder is characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks. A panic attack is an abrupt surge of intense fear or discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes, accompanied by four or more specific physical and cognitive symptoms.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Recurrent unexpected panic attacks.
  • At least one attack followed by 1 month or more of persistent concern about additional attacks/consequences or significant maladaptive change in behavior.
  • Symptoms include palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, choking, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, chills/heat, numbness/tingling, derealization/depersonalization, fear of losing control/"going crazy", fear of dying.

Associated Features:

  • Constant or intermittent feelings of anxiety related to health and mental health.
  • Excessive use of drugs (alcohol, illicit drugs) to control attacks, or extreme behaviors (e.g., severe food restrictions).
  • Worries about panic attacks often relate to physical concerns (e.g., heart attack), social concerns (embarrassment), or mental functioning (going crazy).

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US and Europe is about 2%-3% in adults/adolescents.
  • Women are more frequently affected than men (approx. 2:1 ratio).
  • Risk factors include negative affectivity, anxiety sensitivity, behavioral inhibition, harm avoidance, history of trauma/stress, childhood adversities, parental overprotection, low emotional warmth, and smoking.
  • Multiple genes likely confer vulnerability; increased risk among offspring of parents with anxiety, depressive, and bipolar disorders.

Agoraphobia

Agoraphobia is marked fear or anxiety about two or more of five specific situations: using public transportation, being in open spaces, being in enclosed places, standing in line/being in a crowd, and being outside of the home alone. Individuals fear these situations because escape might be difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms occur.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Marked fear/anxiety about at least two of the five agoraphobic situations.
  • Situations are feared because of thoughts that escape might be difficult or help unavailable.
  • Situations almost always provoke fear/anxiety.
  • Actively avoided, require a companion, or are endured with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual danger.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • In severe forms, can cause individuals to become completely homebound.
  • Demoralization, depressive symptoms, and abuse of alcohol/sedative medication are common.

Prevalence:

  • Approximately 1%-1.7% of adolescents and adults worldwide experience symptoms annually.
  • Women are twice as likely as men to experience agoraphobia.

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Generalized Anxiety Disorder is characterized by excessive anxiety and worry occurring more days than not for at least 6 months, about a number of events or activities. The individual finds it difficult to control the worry, and it is associated with specific physical and cognitive symptoms.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Excessive anxiety and worry for at least 6 months, about multiple events/activities.
  • Difficulty controlling the worry.
  • Associated with three (or more) of six symptoms: restlessness, easily fatigued, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Commonly associated with muscle tension (trembling, twitching, aches).
  • May experience somatic symptoms (sweating, nausea, diarrhea) and an exaggerated startle response.

Prevalence:

  • 12-month prevalence is 0.9% among adolescents and 2.9% among adults in the US.
  • Women and adolescent girls are at least twice as likely as men and adolescent boys to experience GAD.

Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

This category includes Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD), Body Dysmorphic Disorder, Hoarding Disorder, Trichotillomania (Hair-Pulling Disorder), and Excoriation (Skin-Picking) Disorder, among others. These disorders share features of obsessive thoughts and/or repetitive behaviors.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD is characterized by the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both. Obsessions are recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images experienced as intrusive and unwanted, causing distress. Compulsions are repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed in response to an obsession or rigidly applied rules, aimed at preventing or reducing anxiety/distress.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Obsessions: Recurrent/persistent intrusive thoughts, urges, or images; attempts to ignore/suppress them or neutralize with compulsions.
  • Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors (e.g., hand washing, checking) or mental acts (e.g., praying, counting) performed in response to obsessions or rigid rules.
  • Obsessions or compulsions are time-consuming (more than 1 hour/day) or cause clinically significant distress/impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Sensory phenomena (physical sensations, "just-right" feelings) often precede compulsions.
  • Avoidance of triggers. Dysfunctional beliefs like inflated sense of responsibility and overestimation of threats.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is 1.2%.
  • Women are slightly more affected in adulthood; men more common in childhood.
  • Risk factors include greater internalizing symptoms, negative emotionality, behavioral inhibition, adverse perinatal events, childhood abuse/trauma.
  • Genetic factors: approx. 2x risk for first-degree relatives. Dysfunction in orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, striatum implicated.
  • High comorbidity with anxiety and depressive disorders.
  • Associated with suicidal ideation and attempts (mean lifetime suicide attempts of 14.2%).

Psychological Causal Factors:

  • Mowrer's Two-Process Theory: Neutral stimuli associated with frightening thoughts (classical conditioning), and compulsions reduce anxiety (reinforcement).
  • Preparedness: Obsessions about dirt/contamination may have evolutionary roots.
  • Cognitive Factors: Difficulty suppressing unwanted thoughts (paradoxical increase), inflated sense of responsibility, thought-action fusion (thinking a sin is morally equivalent to doing it). Low confidence in memory contributes to repetitive behaviors.
  • Neurotransmitters: Increased serotonin activity and sensitivity of brain structures to serotonin, also involvement of dopaminergic, GABA, and glutamate systems.

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)

Body Dysmorphic Disorder is characterized by a preoccupation with one or more perceived defects or flaws in physical appearance that are not observable or appear only slight to others. This preoccupation leads to repetitive behaviors or mental acts (e.g., mirror checking, excessive grooming, comparing appearance) and causes significant distress or impairment.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Preoccupation with perceived defects/flaws in appearance.
  • Repetitive behaviors or mental acts in response to appearance concerns.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.
  • Not better explained by concerns with body fat/weight (e.g., eating disorder).
  • Can include muscle dysmorphia (preoccupation with being too small/insufficiently muscular).

Associated Features:

  • Intense preoccupation leading to avoidance of activities and social isolation (average employment rate ~50%).
  • Common focus areas: skin, hair, nose, eyes, breasts/chest, stomach.
  • Frequent reassurance-seeking from others or mirror checking (often leads to feeling worse).
  • Excessive grooming, camouflaging perceived defects.
  • Ideas or delusions of reference (believing others notice/mock them).
  • High levels of anxiety, social anxiety, depression, negative affectivity, rejection sensitivity, perfectionism.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • Affects 1-2% of the general population, up to 8% of people with depression.
  • Prevalence is approximately equal in men and women, though focus areas differ (men: genitals, body build, balding; women: skin, stomach, breasts).
  • Onset usually in adolescence.
  • High rates of suicidal ideation (80%) and attempts (28%).
  • Risk factors: childhood neglect, abuse, trauma, teasing.
  • Heritability estimated at 37%-49%; shared genetic vulnerability with OCD.
  • Sociocultural context valuing attractiveness and beauty, and individual self-schemas heavily focused on appearance.

Trichotillomania (Hair-Pulling Disorder)

Trichotillomania is characterized by recurrent pulling out of one's hair, resulting in hair loss, and repeated unsuccessful attempts to decrease or stop hair pulling. It was previously categorized as an impulse-control disorder but is now grouped under obsessive-compulsive and related disorders in DSM-5 due to its relationship with OCD.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Recurrent hair pulling leading to hair loss.
  • Repeated attempts to decrease or stop hair pulling.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • May involve rituals with pulled hair (e.g., specific hair search, visual examination, oral manipulation).
  • Triggered by anxiety or boredom; preceded by increasing tension or followed by gratification/relief.
  • Varying degrees of conscious awareness (focused vs. automatic).
  • Often occurs privately, but sometimes with immediate family.
  • Some individuals pull hair from pets, dolls, or fibrous materials.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the USA ranges from 1% to 2%.
  • Women are more frequently affected than men (though some surveys show similar rates).
  • Evidence for genetic vulnerability; more common in individuals with OCD and their first-degree relatives.
  • Frequently accompanied by major depressive disorder and excoriation (skin-picking) disorder.

Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders

This category includes Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), characterized by a specific set of symptoms that develop following exposure to a traumatic event.

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD involves exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. Symptoms fall into several clusters:

  • Intrusion Symptoms: Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the event; recurrent distressing dreams; dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks); intense/prolonged psychological distress or physiological reactions to trauma-related cues.
  • Avoidance: Persistent avoidance of distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about the event, or external reminders (people, places, conversations, objects, situations).
  • Negative Alterations in Cognitions and Mood: Inability to remember aspects of the event, negative beliefs/expectations about oneself/others/world, distorted cognitions about cause/consequence of event leading to self-blame, negative emotional state, diminished interest, detachment, anhedonia.
  • Alterations in Arousal and Reactivity: Irritable behavior/angry outbursts, reckless/self-destructive behavior, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, problems with concentration, sleep disturbance.
  • Symptoms must last for more than 1 month and cause clinically significant distress or impairment.
