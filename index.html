Introduction to Abnormal Psychology

Abnormal psychology is the field dedicated to understanding the *nature, causes, and treatment of mental disorders*. A psychological disorder is fundamentally a psychological dysfunction within an individual associated with significant distress or impairment, and a response that is not typical or culturally expected.

Defining Abnormality & Psychological Disorder:

  • Psychological Dysfunction: A breakdown in cognitive, emotional, or behavioral functioning. Importantly, many disorders are extreme expressions of normal processes.
  • Distress or Impairment: The behavior must cause significant emotional pain or interfere with daily functioning (e.g., social, occupational). However, distress alone isn't sufficient.
  • Atypical/Culturally Unexpected: The response deviates from cultural norms. Predictable responses to stressors (like grief) are excluded. The line between normal and abnormal is often a continuum.

Key Indicators of Abnormality:

While no single factor defines abnormality, common indicators include:

  1. Suffering: Experiencing psychological pain (though not always present, e.g., during mania).
  2. Maladaptiveness: Behavior that interferes with well-being or societal functioning (e.g., anorexia, antisocial behavior).
  3. Statistical Deviancy: Statistically rare behavior (e.g., intellectual disability, though not genius). Value judgments play a role here.
  4. Violation of Social Standards: Failing to follow societal rules, especially if severe or uncommon.
  5. Social Discomfort: Causing unease in others (e.g., inappropriate self-disclosure).
  6. Irrationality and Unpredictability: Behavior that is inexplicable and uncontrollable (e.g., disorganized speech in schizophrenia).
  7. Dangerousness: Being a danger to oneself or others (though not all dangerous individuals are mentally ill, and vice-versa).

Historical Perspectives on Abnormal Behavior

Throughout history, the understanding and treatment of abnormal behavior have shifted dramatically, from supernatural beliefs to scientific inquiry.

Supernatural Tradition:

  • Demons and Witches: Early explanations often attributed deviant behavior to evil spirits, demons, or witchcraft. Treatments involved exorcism, prayers, and sometimes harsh punishments (e.g., the Salem witch trials).
  • Stress & Melancholy: Some ancient views, like that of Nicholas Oresme, saw insanity as a natural phenomenon stemming from mental/emotional stress, advocating rest and healthy environments.
  • Mass Hysteria: Phenomena like Saint Vitus's Dance (group compulsion to dance) were seen, possibly due to emotional contagion and suggestibility.
  • Moon & Stars: Paracelsus suggested lunar and stellar movements affected psychological functioning, giving rise to the term "lunatic."

Biological Tradition:

  • Hippocrates & Galen: Hippocrates, the "father of modern medicine," denied supernatural causes, positing that psychological disorders resulted from brain pathology, head trauma, or genetics. He introduced the *humoral theory* (imbalance of blood, black bile, yellow bile, phlegm). Galen further developed these ideas.
  • The 19th Century & Syphilis: The discovery that advanced syphilis caused "general paresis" (psychotic symptoms) and was curable (initially via malaria, later penicillin) significantly boosted the belief that all psychological disorders might have biological roots.
  • John P. Grey: A prominent American psychiatrist who advocated for the physical basis of insanity, leading to improved but also larger, more impersonal mental hospitals focused on rest, diet, and proper ventilation.
  • Biological Treatments: The 1930s saw the emergence of electric shock (ECT) and insulin shock therapy. The 1950s revolutionized treatment with the discovery of *neuroleptics* (for psychosis) and *benzodiazepines* (for anxiety).
  • Emil Kraepelin: A key figure in modern psychiatry, he focused on *diagnosis and classification*, distinguishing disorders by onset, course, symptoms, and probable causes, laying groundwork for modern diagnostic systems.

Psychological Tradition:

  • Early Philosophers: Plato emphasized social and cultural influences, suggesting rational discussion as a treatment. Aristotle also highlighted the impact of the social environment.
  • Moral Therapy (19th Century): Advocated for humane treatment of institutionalized patients, encouraging normal social interaction. Key figures included Philippe Pinel, Jean-Baptiste Pussin, and William Tuke. This approach declined due to increasing patient loads and the prevailing biological view.
  • Psychoanalytic Theory (Sigmund Freud):
    • Unconscious Mind: Through work with Josef Breuer, Freud "discovered" that unconscious conflicts and past emotional traumas could influence psychological disorders.
    • Structure of the Mind: Proposed the *id* (pleasure principle), *ego* (reality principle, mediator), and *superego* (moral conscience).
    • Defense Mechanisms: Unconscious strategies (e.g., denial, repression, projection) used by the ego to manage anxiety arising from intrapsychic conflicts.
    • Psychosexual Stages: Argued personality develops through oral, anal, phallic, latency, and genital stages, with fixation at any stage influencing adult personality.
    • Psychoanalytic Psychotherapy: Techniques like free association and dream analysis aimed to uncover unconscious conflicts and promote *catharsis* (emotional release) and *insight*.
  • Humanistic Theory (Carl Rogers, Abraham Maslow): Emphasized the positive nature of humanity, focusing on self-actualization and reaching one's full potential. Rogers developed *client-centered therapy*, stressing unconditional positive regard and empathy.
  • Behavioral Model (Pavlov, Watson, Skinner): A scientific approach focusing on observable behavior and learning processes.
    • Classical Conditioning (Ivan Pavlov): Learning by association, where a neutral stimulus elicits a response after being paired with an unconditioned stimulus.
    • Behaviorism (John B. Watson): Rejected introspection, focusing on predicting and controlling behavior (e.g., Little Albert experiment).
    • Operant Conditioning (B. F. Skinner): Behavior is shaped by its consequences (reinforcement, punishment), with an emphasis on positive reinforcement.
    • Systematic Desensitization (Joseph Wolpe): A behavioral therapy technique for phobias, involving gradual exposure to feared stimuli while practicing relaxation.

Classification of Mental Disorders

The classification of mental disorders is fundamental for diagnosis, research, and treatment. The two primary systems are the *International Classification of Diseases (ICD)* and the *Diagnostic & Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM)*.

International Classification of Diseases (ICD):

  • Publisher: World Health Organization (WHO).
  • Scope: Comprehensive, covering all diseases, including a dedicated chapter for mental and behavioral disorders (ICD-11).
  • Usage: Globally used by governments and health systems for clinical diagnosis, epidemiological tracking, and health management, ensuring consistency worldwide.
  • Availability: Broadly distributed, often low cost or free.

Diagnostic & Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM):

  • Publisher: American Psychiatric Association (APA).
  • Scope: Exclusively focuses on mental disorders.
  • Criteria: Provides detailed, specific, symptom-based diagnostic criteria for each condition, aiding precise diagnoses.
  • Context: Primarily developed within a Western context.
  • Revenue: Generates revenue for the APA.

Need for Classification:

  • Standardization: Provides a common language and framework for professionals globally, reducing ambiguity.
  • Accurate Diagnosis & Treatment: Facilitates matching symptoms to criteria, leading to precise diagnoses and appropriate treatment plans.
  • Facilitating Research: Ensures consistency in defining disorders, enabling comparable data, clinical trials, and epidemiological studies.
  • Policy Development & Resource Allocation: Informs public health statistics, helping governments plan services and funding.
  • Insurance & Reimbursement: Official diagnostic codes are often required by health insurance providers for billing.
  • Global Comparability & Cultural Adaptation: Allows comparison of mental health trends across nations and adaptation to cultural differences in symptom expression.

Overview of Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety disorders are characterized by excessive fear and anxiety, coupled with related behavioral disturbances. They differ in the types of objects or situations that trigger fear, anxiety, or avoidance behaviors, and the associated thoughts.

  • Fear: Emotional response to immediate threat (fight-or-flight).
  • Anxiety: Anticipation of future threat (muscle tension, vigilance, avoidance).

Specific Phobia

Marked fear or anxiety about a specific object or situation (e.g., flying, heights, animals, injections, blood). The fear is often disproportionate to the actual danger and significantly interferes with daily life.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Marked fear/anxiety about a specific object or situation.
  • The phobic stimulus almost always provokes immediate fear/anxiety.
  • Active avoidance or endurance with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual danger.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Increased physiological arousal (except for blood-injection-injury type, which may cause vasovagal fainting).
  • Associated with abnormal activity in the amygdala, anterior cingulate cortex, thalamus, and insula.
  • Comorbidity with other anxiety, depressive, bipolar, substance-related, somatic symptom, and personality disorders.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is 8%-12%; generally lower in Asian, African, Latin American countries.
  • Women are more frequently affected (approx. 2:1 ratio).
  • Onset usually in early childhood (median 7-11 years).
  • Risk factors include negative affectivity, behavioral inhibition, parental overprotectiveness, and genetic susceptibility (e.g., specific phobia of animals).
  • Associated with suicidal thoughts and attempts.

Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)

Social Anxiety Disorder is characterized by marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations where the individual is exposed to possible scrutiny by others. The fear stems from concern about negative evaluation (e.g., humiliating or embarrassing oneself).

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Fear/anxiety in social situations involving scrutiny (e.g., conversations, eating, performing).
  • Fear of acting in a way or showing anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated.
  • Social situations almost always provoke fear/anxiety.
  • Avoided or endured with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual threat.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Individuals may be inadequately assertive, overly submissive, or overly controlling of conversation.
  • May show rigid body posture, inadequate eye contact, or soft voice.
  • Self-medication with substances is common. Blushing is a hallmark physical response.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is approximately 7%. Higher rates in women than men in the general population.
  • Onset does not differ by gender, but women report more fears and comorbidity.
  • Risk factors include behavioral inhibition, fear of negative evaluation, harm avoidance, negative social experiences (e.g., peer victimization, childhood maltreatment).
  • Modest genetic contribution (approx. 30% heritability).
  • Associated with elevated rates of school dropout, decreased well-being, employment, and quality of life.

Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder is characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks. A panic attack is an abrupt surge of intense fear or discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes, accompanied by four or more specific physical and cognitive symptoms.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Recurrent unexpected panic attacks.
  • At least one attack followed by 1 month or more of persistent concern about additional attacks/consequences or significant maladaptive change in behavior.
  • Symptoms include palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, choking, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, chills/heat, numbness/tingling, derealization/depersonalization, fear of losing control/"going crazy", fear of dying.

Associated Features:

  • Constant or intermittent feelings of anxiety related to health and mental health.
  • Excessive use of drugs (alcohol, illicit drugs) to control attacks, or extreme behaviors (e.g., severe food restrictions).
  • Worries about panic attacks often relate to physical concerns (e.g., heart attack), social concerns (embarrassment), or mental functioning (going crazy).

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US and Europe is about 2%-3% in adults/adolescents.
  • Women are more frequently affected than men (approx. 2:1 ratio).
  • Risk factors include negative affectivity, anxiety sensitivity, behavioral inhibition, harm avoidance, history of trauma/stress, childhood adversities, parental overprotection, low emotional warmth, and smoking.
  • Multiple genes likely confer vulnerability; increased risk among offspring of parents with anxiety, depressive, and bipolar disorders.

Agoraphobia

Agoraphobia is marked fear or anxiety about two or more of five specific situations: public transportation, open spaces, enclosed places, standing in line/being in a crowd, and being outside of the home alone. Individuals fear these situations because escape might be difficult or help unavailable if panic-like symptoms occur.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Marked fear/anxiety about at least two of the five agoraphobic situations.
  • Situations are feared because of thoughts that escape might be difficult or help unavailable.
  • Situations almost always provoke fear/anxiety.
  • Actively avoided, require a companion, or are endured with intense fear/anxiety.
  • Fear/anxiety is out of proportion to actual danger.
  • Persistent, typically lasting 6 months or more.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • In severe forms, can cause individuals to become completely homebound.
  • Demoralization, depressive symptoms, and abuse of alcohol/sedative medication are common.

Prevalence:

  • Approximately 1%-1.7% of adolescents and adults worldwide experience symptoms annually.
  • Women are twice as likely as men to experience agoraphobia.

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Generalized Anxiety Disorder is characterized by excessive anxiety and worry occurring more days than not for at least 6 months, about a number of events or activities. The individual finds it difficult to control the worry, and it is associated with specific physical and cognitive symptoms.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Excessive anxiety and worry for at least 6 months, about multiple events/activities.
  • Difficulty controlling the worry.
  • Associated with three (or more) of six symptoms: restlessness, easily fatigued, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Commonly associated with muscle tension (trembling, twitching, aches).
  • May experience somatic symptoms (sweating, nausea, diarrhea) and an exaggerated startle response.

Prevalence:

  • 12-month prevalence is 0.9% among adolescents and 2.9% among adults in the US.
  • Women and adolescent girls are at least twice as likely as men and adolescent boys to experience GAD.

Separation Anxiety Disorder

Separation Anxiety Disorder involves developmentally inappropriate and excessive fear or anxiety concerning separation from major attachment figures. This goes beyond typical clinginess.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights (at least 3 symptoms):

  • Recurrent excessive distress when anticipating or experiencing separation.
  • Persistent, excessive worry about losing or harming attachment figures.
  • Persistent worry about untoward events causing separation (e.g., getting lost, kidnapping).
  • Persistent reluctance/refusal to go out, to school/work, or elsewhere due to separation fear.
  • Persistent, excessive fear or reluctance about being alone or without attachment figures.
  • Persistent reluctance/refusal to sleep away from home or without being near an attachment figure.
  • Repeated nightmares involving separation.
  • Repeated physical complaints (e.g., headaches, stomachaches) when separation occurs/is anticipated.
  • Symptoms last ≥4 weeks in children/adolescents, ≥6 months in adults. Causes clinically significant distress/impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Children may display "clinging" behavior, needing someone with them even in another room.
  • Difficulty at bedtime, insisting someone stays until they fall asleep.
  • May make their way to parents' bed during the night.

Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

This category includes conditions characterized by *obsessions* (recurrent, intrusive thoughts/urges/images) and/or *compulsions* (repetitive behaviors/mental acts performed in response to obsessions or rigid rules).

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Defined by the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both. Obsessions cause distress, and compulsions are aimed at neutralizing anxiety/distress. Symptoms are time-consuming (≥1 hour/day) or cause significant distress/impairment.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Obsessions: Recurrent/persistent intrusive thoughts, urges, or images; attempts to ignore/suppress them or neutralize with compulsions.
  • Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors (e.g., hand washing, checking) or mental acts (e.g., praying, counting) performed in response to obsessions or rigid rules.
  • Obsessions or compulsions are time-consuming (more than 1 hour/day) or cause clinically significant distress/impairment.

Associated Features:

  • Sensory phenomena (physical sensations, "just-right" feelings) often precede compulsions.
  • Avoidance of triggers. Dysfunctional beliefs like inflated sense of responsibility and overestimation of threats.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the US is 1.2%.
  • Women are slightly more affected in adulthood; men more common in childhood.
  • Risk factors include internalizing symptoms, negative emotionality, behavioral inhibition, adverse perinatal events, childhood abuse/trauma.
  • Genetic factors: approx. 2x risk for first-degree relatives. Dysfunction in orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, striatum implicated.
  • High comorbidity with anxiety and depressive disorders.
  • Associated with suicidal ideation (44.1% lifetime) and attempts (14.2% lifetime).

Causal Factors for OCD:

  • Mowrer's Two-Process Theory: Classical conditioning links neutral stimuli to fear (e.g., doorknobs to contamination), and operant conditioning reinforces compulsions (e.g., hand washing reduces anxiety).
  • Preparedness: Evolutionary roots for obsessions about dirt/contamination.
  • Cognitive Factors: Difficulty suppressing unwanted thoughts (paradoxical increase), inflated sense of responsibility, *thought-action fusion* (thinking a bad thought is morally equivalent to doing it), low memory confidence leading to repetitive checking.
  • Neurotransmitters: Implicated serotonin activity and sensitivity; also involvement of dopamine, GABA, and glutamate systems.

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)

Preoccupation with ≥1 perceived defects/flaws in physical appearance that are unobservable or slight to others. This leads to repetitive behaviors (e.g., mirror checking, excessive grooming, comparing appearance) or mental acts (e.g., comparing appearance) and causes significant distress or impairment.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Preoccupation with perceived defects/flaws in appearance.
  • Repetitive behaviors or mental acts in response to appearance concerns.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.
  • Not better explained by concerns with body fat/weight (e.g., eating disorder).
  • Can include *muscle dysmorphia* (preoccupation with being too small/insufficiently muscular).

Associated Features:

  • Intense preoccupation leading to avoidance of activities and social isolation (average employment rate ~50%).
  • Common focus areas: skin, hair, nose, eyes, breasts/chest, stomach.
  • Frequent reassurance-seeking from others or mirror checking (often leads to feeling worse).
  • Excessive grooming, camouflaging perceived defects.
  • Ideas or delusions of reference (believing others notice/mock them).
  • High levels of anxiety, social anxiety, depression, negative affectivity, rejection sensitivity, perfectionism.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • Affects 1-2% of the general population, up to 8% of people with depression.
  • Prevalence is approximately equal in men and women, though focus areas differ (men: genitals, body build, balding; women: skin, stomach, breasts).
  • Onset usually in adolescence.
  • High rates of suicidal ideation (80%) and attempts (28%).
  • Risk factors: childhood neglect, abuse, trauma, teasing.
  • Heritability estimated at 37%-49%; shared genetic vulnerability with OCD.
  • Sociocultural context valuing attractiveness and beauty, and individual self-schemas heavily focused on appearance.

Trichotillomania (Hair-Pulling Disorder)

Recurrent pulling out of one's hair, resulting in noticeable hair loss, despite repeated attempts to stop. Causes clinically significant distress or impairment. Previously an impulse-control disorder, now grouped under obsessive-compulsive and related disorders in DSM-5 due to its relationship with OCD.

Diagnostic Criteria Highlights:

  • Recurrent hair pulling leading to hair loss.
  • Repeated attempts to decrease or stop hair pulling.
  • Causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Associated Features:

  • May involve rituals with pulled hair (e.g., specific hair search, visual examination, oral manipulation).
  • Triggered by anxiety or boredom; preceded by increasing tension or followed by gratification/relief.
  • Varying degrees of conscious awareness (focused vs. automatic).
  • Often occurs privately, but sometimes with immediate family.
  • Some individuals pull hair from pets, dolls, or fibrous materials.

Prevalence & Risk Factors:

  • 12-month prevalence in the USA ranges from 1% to 2%.
  • Women are more frequently affected than men (though some surveys show similar rates).
  • Evidence for genetic vulnerability; more common in individuals with OCD and their first-degree relatives.
  • Frequently accompanied by major depressive disorder and excoriation (skin-picking) disorder.

Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders: Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD is a disorder that can develop following exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. It is characterized by symptoms lasting for more than one month and causing clinically significant distress or impairment.

Key Symptom Clusters:

  • Intrusion Symptoms: Recurrent, involuntary, and distressing memories; recurrent distressing dreams; dissociative reactions (flashbacks); intense psychological distress or physiological reactions to trauma cues.
  • Avoidance: Persistent efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, feelings, or external reminders related to the traumatic event.
  • Negative Alterations in Cognitions and Mood: Inability to recall key aspects of the trauma; persistent negative beliefs about self, others, or the world; distorted thoughts about the cause/consequence of the event (leading to self-blame); negative emotional state (fear, horror, anger, guilt, shame); diminished interest in activities; feelings of detachment from others; inability to experience positive emotions (anhedonia).
  • Alterations in Arousal and Reactivity: Irritable behavior and angry outbursts; reckless or self-destructive behavior; hypervigilance; exaggerated startle response; problems with concentration; sleep disturbance.
